My Trading Strategy Journey — IMC Prosperity 4 (Tutorial Round)
================================================================
Products traded: EMERALDS (stationary) | TOMATOES (drifting, unknown fair value)

This document walks through the four iterations of my trading algorithm, describing
the thought process, the improvements, and the lessons learned at each stage.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  TRADER (1) — First Attempt: ML-Inspired Heuristics
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

My starting point was intuition: I asked myself what signals I would use if I were
trying to predict short-term price movements in a fast-moving market.

For EMERALDS, I knew the fair value was around 10,000 (visible from the data), so
the strategy was straightforward: make markets around that price, take any order
that was clearly mispriced, and skew my passive quotes against my inventory to
avoid accumulating too much directional risk.

For TOMATOES, the fair value was unknown and drifting, so I assembled three signals:

  1. Exponential Moving Average (EMA) as my fair value estimate.
     I replaced the naive simple moving average (SMA) with an EMA because I knew
     that for drifting price series, recent observations should matter more than old
     ones. The SMA weights every point in the window equally — that seemed wrong
     intuitively. I chose alpha = 0.25 by feel.

  2. Linear regression slope to capture trending behaviour.
     I ran an OLS regression of recent midprices against a time index over a rolling
     30-tick window. If the slope is positive, the price is trending up, so I raise
     my fair value estimate slightly; if negative, I lower it. I project this slope
     forward by 3 ticks (the "horizon") and blend it into my fair value.

  3. Order Book Imbalance (OBI) as a short-term directional signal.
     OBI = (total bid volume − total ask volume) / (total bid + total ask volume).
     A positive OBI means more buy pressure on the book, suggesting prices will rise;
     negative means sell pressure. I nudge my passive quote midpoint in that direction.

  4. Inventory skew to manage position risk.
     If I am long, I lower my bid and ask prices slightly to make selling more
     attractive; if short, I raise them. This prevents runaway inventory accumulation.

The general structure — aggressive phase (take mispriced orders) followed by passive
phase (post limit orders around fair value) — was established here and kept in all
subsequent versions.

What I was not happy with: every parameter (alpha, window, weights) was a guess.
I had no principled way to know if alpha=0.25 was better or worse than 0.3. The
linear regression slope added noise for a process that might be a random walk.
I had no formal understanding of what statistical process TOMATOES actually followed.
This motivated a complete rethink.

  TRADER (2) — Econometric Foundation: Let the Data Speak
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

I went back to first principles and applied the econometric tools I had studied in
my LSE Metrics II course (Slide Packs #3 and #6) directly to the price data.

For EMERALDS:
  I ran a Dickey-Fuller (DF) unit root test on the price series. The t-statistic
  came out at approximately -97, which is an overwhelming rejection of the unit
  root hypothesis. This means EMERALDS is strongly stationary — it reverts to a
  fixed mean. I then checked the autocorrelation function (ACF) of the levels and
  found ACF(1) ≈ 0.03, essentially zero. This tells me the series is white noise
  around its mean of 10,000. The implication for trading is clear: the fair value
  is 10,000 with near-certainty. There is no point building a complex forecasting
  model; I just need to make markets around 10,000 as efficiently as possible.

For TOMATOES:
  The ACF of the levels showed ACF(1) ≈ 0.99, a classic near-unit-root signature —
  prices are highly persistent and drift over time. I then differenced the series
  (computed ΔY_t = Y_t − Y_{t-1}) and examined the ACF of the differences. I found:
    — ACF(1) of ΔY_t ≈ −0.41   (significant negative autocorrelation at lag 1)
    — ACF(2), ACF(3), ... ≈ 0   (cuts off sharply after lag 1)
  This ACF pattern — one significant lag then cutoff — is the textbook signature of
  a Moving Average process of order 1 in the differences. In other words, TOMATOES
  follows an ARIMA(0,1,1) process:
      ΔY_t = ε_t + θ · ε_{t-1}
  where θ ≈ −0.55 (estimated from ACF(1): θ ≈ ACF(1) / (1 + ACF(1)^2 ≈ ...) ).

  The critical insight: for an ARIMA(0,1,1) process, the theoretically optimal
  1-step-ahead forecast is an Exponentially Weighted Moving Average with smoothing
  parameter α = 1 + θ ≈ 1 − 0.55 = 0.45 (I used 0.43 after more careful fitting).
  This is not an arbitrary choice — it is derived from the Kalman filter / EWMA
  equivalence for MA(1) processes. I verified empirically that EMA(0.43) reduces
  forecast MSE by approximately 53% compared to SMA(20).

I also introduced the microprice as a better fair-value input than the raw midprice:
  microprice = (best_bid × ask_vol + best_ask × bid_vol) / (bid_vol + ask_vol)
This volume-weights the two sides of the book. If the ask side is heavier, the
microprice sits closer to the ask — a more accurate reflection of where the market
is likely to trade next.

This version also introduced inventory skewing and adaptive spread widening based
on rolling volatility estimates. However, in the implementation I introduced four
subtle bugs that reduced performance — these were identified and fixed in v3.

  TRADER (3) — Systematic Debugging: Rigour in Execution
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Even a theoretically sound strategy fails if it is implemented incorrectly. After
backtesting v2 and studying the logs carefully, I identified four bugs:

  BUG 1 — Aggressive fills used the skewed fair value for TOMATOES.
  At maximum inventory (position = 80), a skew of 0.15 per unit = 12 ticks.
  The aggressive threshold was checking: is this ask below (fair − 12)?
  This means I was refusing to buy at prices like fair − 11, fair − 5, fair − 1,
  all of which are genuinely profitable. I was leaving money on the table.
  FIX: aggressive fills always compare against the raw (unskewed) EMA fair value.

  BUG 2 — Inventory skew coefficient was too large for TOMATOES (0.15 per unit).
  At the position limit of 80, this produced 12 ticks of skew. The typical spread
  was only 4 ticks total. This meant my passive sell was priced 10 ticks below fair
  — I was gifting edge to counterparties. The skew should be small enough to stay
  comfortably inside the spread.
  FIX: TOMATO_SKEW reduced from 0.15 to 0.04 per unit → max 3.2 ticks at limit.

  BUG 3 — The adaptive spread was uncapped, growing very wide in volatile periods.
  With vol_adj up to 2 and inv_adj up to 2, the half-spread could reach 6 ticks
  (full spread 12 ticks). This was wider than the entire visible order book, meaning
  my passive quotes never got filled. The spread was supposed to adapt, not collapse.
  FIX: hard cap on half-spread at 4 ticks.

  BUG 4 — Aggressive fills for EMERALDS also used the skewed fair value.
  EMERALDS fair value is known with certainty to be 10,000. There is no reason to
  refuse buying at 9,999 just because I happen to be long at that moment. The skew
  on aggressive fills was causing me to miss profitable arbitrage opportunities.
  FIX: aggressive EMERALDS fills always use raw fair = 10,000; skew only applied
  to passive quote prices.

The core principle I established: the aggressive phase and passive phase must be
decoupled. Aggressive fills capture certain edge (mispriced orders) and should
never be skewed. Passive quotes manage inventory risk and can be skewed. Conflating
the two costs real PnL.

  TRADER (4) — Final: Market Microstructure Innovation
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

With a correct, theory-grounded implementation in place, I turned to the question
of how to compete more effectively against other market participants. I introduced
two new ideas from market microstructure theory:

  INNOVATION 1 — Book-Adaptive Pennying (queue-jumping).

  Instead of posting passive quotes at a fixed spread from fair value (e.g., always
  at fair ± 2), I read the actual live order book each tick to see where competitor
  bots are resting their passive orders. I then post 1 tick inside their best price.

  For example, if the best competing bid is at 9,997 and fair is 10,000:
    — A fixed ±2 strategy posts at 9,998 (same or worse)
    — My pennying strategy posts at 9,998 (1 tick above 9,997) to gain queue priority

  This is observation-driven: zero hyperparameters, zero risk of overfitting to
  historical data. It purely reacts to the current market structure. The only
  constraints are hard floors/ceilings so I never post at or across fair value
  (which would eliminate my edge). I applied this to both EMERALDS and TOMATOES.

  INNOVATION 2 — Two-Level Passive Layering.

  Instead of posting my entire remaining capacity at a single price level, I split it:
    Layer 1: 60% at the penny price      → tight price, queue priority, smaller edge
    Layer 2: 40% at penny price ± 1 tick → one tick further, larger edge per fill

  The motivation: if competitor bots tighten their spread (post at ±1 instead of ±2),
  a pure penny strategy suffers badly — you end up at fair ± 1 with zero edge.
  I simulated this scenario:
    — Fixed ±2 strategy when bots penny to ±1: near-zero fills (catastrophic).
    — Pure penny strategy when bots at ±1: posts at fair ± 0, negative edge.
    — Two-level strategy: layer 2 still has meaningful edge regardless of what bots do.
  The two-level split is more robust across the range of possible competitor behaviours.
  It sacrifices some PnL in the best case but avoids catastrophic failure in edge cases.

  The final version also carries over all bug fixes and econometric foundations from v3:
    — Aggressive / passive separation (raw fair for fills, skewed fair for quotes)
    — EMA(0.43) of microprice as the ARIMA(0,1,1)-optimal forecast for TOMATOES
    — Inventory skew on passive quotes only (max 2 ticks at position limit)
    — Spread widens by 1 tick when rolling volatility σ(ΔY) > 2.5

  KEY LESSONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1. Understand your data before choosing a model. Running formal statistical tests
     (DF test, ACF analysis) told me exactly what kind of process each product
     followed — and therefore exactly what the optimal forecasting strategy was.
     Intuition-based model selection (v1) leaves parameters ungrounded.

  2. Separate concerns in implementation. Aggressive fills and passive quoting serve
     different purposes; mixing them (e.g., applying inventory skew to aggressive
     fills) causes silent bugs that are hard to detect without careful log analysis.

  3. Small bugs, large PnL impact. Bug 1 alone (wrong threshold for aggressive fills)
     was causing me to miss fills at prices that were genuinely profitable. These
     kinds of implementation errors do not throw exceptions — they just quietly reduce
     performance, which makes them particularly dangerous.

  4. Robustness over optimisation. The two-level layering in v4 does not maximise
     PnL in any single scenario; it minimises the worst case across scenarios. In
     live competition where competitor behaviour is unknown, this robustness matters
     more than squeezing out the last basis point under idealistic assumptions.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  TRADER (5) — Momentum Signals: Dual-EMA and Urgency Skew
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

With v4 establishing a correct and well-structured baseline, I focused on
handling trending markets better. My backtests on both training days revealed
a common failure mode: in a prolonged downtrend, the single EMA(α=0.43) lags
behind the falling price. My passive buy orders would fill repeatedly at prices
above the true current fair value, building long inventory precisely when I
should have been reducing it.

Before settling on my final approach, I tested and discarded three candidates:

  DISCARDED — At-fair EMERALDS aggressive fills.
  I considered buying at asks priced exactly at fair (= 10,000) rather than
  strictly below it. The logic seemed simple: fill at 10,000, sell passively
  at 10,007 = +7 ticks. But this ignores opportunity cost: the capacity used
  for that at-fair fill could instead complete a passive round-trip (buy at
  9,993, sell at 10,007) = +14 ticks. Net: -7 ticks per unit of displaced
  capacity. Backtesting confirmed: -245 EMERALDS PnL over 2 training days.

  DISCARDED — Full-book microprice.
  Instead of using only the top level of the order book (best bid/ask volumes)
  for the microprice, I tried weighting all visible levels. The result was
  noisier — on 3.6% of ticks the full-book microprice differed from the level-1
  version by more than 1 tick, occasionally misdirecting the EMA. Across both
  training days, this caused a -160 PnL baseline regression.

  DISCARDED — 1-tick EMA velocity.
  The simplest momentum signal: ema_velocity = EMA_t − EMA_{t-1}. It improved
  performance on the trending day (+138 TOMATOES) but hurt performance on the
  flat/volatile day (-131 TOMATOES). Net: roughly neutral. The problem is that
  1-tick differences in EMA are dominated by noise — they fire on every single
  tick even when there is no genuine trend.

The retained innovations:

  INNOVATION 3 — Dual-EMA Momentum Signal.
  Instead of a 1-tick velocity, I run two EMAs simultaneously:
    ema_fast (α=0.43): already the fair-value estimate — unchanged from v4.
    ema_slow (α=0.10): a slower tracker with effective memory of ~10 ticks.
  The trend signal = ema_fast − ema_slow.

  Why this is better than 1-tick velocity:
    In a sustained downtrend, ema_fast gradually pulls below ema_slow over
    20–30 ticks, building a persistent and growing negative signal. In a flat
    or noisy market, both EMAs orbit the same level and the signal stays near
    zero. The dual-EMA signal only fires when there is a genuine and sustained
    trend — exactly when it should.

  I apply this via a "biased position":
    biased_pos = position − MOMENTUM_BIAS × trend_signal
    where MOMENTUM_BIAS = 5 (position units per tick of trend signal).

    Example — Day -2 downtrend (signal grows to −10 over 40 ticks):
      biased_pos = position + 50.
      This inflates our effective inventory, pushing us deeper into the urgency
      zone and triggering heavier skew. Our passive quotes shift down in the
      direction of the trend — selling lower and buying lower, reducing
      against-trend inventory accumulation.

    Flat / volatile day: signal stays near 0 → biased_pos ≈ position → PnL
      essentially identical to v4. No spurious momentum bias.

  INNOVATION 4 — Urgency Position Skew.
  When |biased_pos| exceeds 70% of the position limit (56 units), the base
  inventory skew is multiplied by 2×. In a strong downtrend with a real long
  position, the momentum bias and the urgency multiplier compound: we get very
  aggressive downward quote skewing when we need it most. This prevents quietly
  drifting into the position limit without reacting.

  INNOVATION 5 — Exponential Moving Variance.
  The rolling-list variance estimate from v4 (20-element window) is replaced
  with a recursive exponential formula:
    emv_t = 0.15 × diff² + 0.85 × emv_{t-1}
  This has an effective memory of ~7 ticks (versus 20), detecting volatility
  regime changes faster. It also eliminates the list from persistent state,
  simplifying the data structure. In practice the vol threshold (2.5 ticks) is
  almost never crossed on tutorial round data, so the main benefit here is
  cleaner code rather than a measurable PnL improvement.

EMERALDS: unchanged from v4 throughout.

  TRADER (6) — Trend-Carry: Embedding the Signal in Fair Value Itself
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Reviewing v5, I noticed a structural inconsistency: the momentum signal
correctly adjusted the passive quote skew, but it had no effect on the fair
value anchor itself. In a downtrend, the aggressive threshold and the passive
quote anchor were still the raw EMA — which by design lags the true current
price. The signal was telling me "price is falling" but I was still buying
aggressively up to the lagged, too-high EMA. This version corrects that.

  INNOVATION 6 — Trend-Carry Fair Value Adjustment.
  I add a small fraction of the trend signal directly into the fair value:
    forecast_ema = ema + TOMATO_TREND_CARRY × trend_signal
    where TOMATO_TREND_CARRY = 0.25 (25% of the signal).

  This forecast_ema is used as the threshold for aggressive fills and as the
  anchor for passive quotes (before inventory skew is applied):
    Downtrend (signal = −10): forecast_ema = ema − 2.5 ticks.
      We stop buying at asks we would have taken in v5 — those slightly above
      the momentum-adjusted fair value. Passive quotes also shift down with
      the trend, following the price rather than lagging behind it.
    Uptrend: symmetric — forecast_ema rises, making us more willing to buy
      near the rising price and less willing to sell into it.

  I also reduced the slow EMA alpha from 0.10 (v5) to 0.06 (v6). At 0.10,
  the slow EMA reacted to intra-day noise on the flat training day, generating
  spurious trend signals. At 0.06 (effective memory ≈ 17 ticks), it only
  reacts to genuine, multi-tick sustained trends.

  INNOVATION 7 — Against-Trend Passive Capacity Throttling.
  Even with the carry adjustment, I was still advertising full passive buy
  capacity in a downtrend. If those passive bids fill, I accumulate long
  inventory against a trend I can observe directly. There is no good reason
  to offer the full book depth in the losing direction.

  Solution: when |trend_signal| exceeds a threshold (1.5 ticks), reduce
  passive size in the against-trend direction to 65% of normal:
    Downtrend (signal < −1.5): cap passive buy capacity to 65%.
    Uptrend (signal > +1.5): cap passive sell capacity to 65%.

  The 35% reduction meaningfully reduces adverse inventory accumulation
  while keeping 65% of spread income available in case the trend reverses
  suddenly. A complete shutdown would be too aggressive: the trend signal
  has magnitude ~2–10 ticks in practice, and reversals happen without warning.

EMERALDS: unchanged from v4/v5 throughout.

  KEY LESSONS (updated)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1–4. (See above, unchanged.)

  5. Apply directional signals consistently throughout the strategy, not
     just at one layer. In v5, the momentum signal adjusted the passive skew
     but left the aggressive threshold and fair-value anchor untouched. The
     result was an internal inconsistency: the signal said "price is falling"
     but the fair value said "fair is where the lagged EMA is." v6 fixed this
     by carrying the signal into forecast_ema, which is then used everywhere.

  6. Control capacity, not just price. A good strategy prices quotes correctly
     and also decides how much it is willing to trade in each direction. Against-
     trend throttling in v6 reduces adverse inventory accumulation by cutting
     passive offer depth on the losing side — without abandoning the spread-
     earning strategy that generates the bulk of PnL. Sizing is risk management.

  7. Test and discard explicitly. v5 documented three discarded approaches
     (at-fair fills, full-book microprice, 1-tick velocity). Keeping a record
     of what failed — and why — prevents revisiting the same dead ends and
     clarifies exactly what each retained change is contributing.

  ROUND 1 — NEW PRODUCTS, NEW DRIFTS: PEPPER & OSMIUM
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

After the tutorial round I moved into Round 1, which replaced
EMERALDS / TOMATOES with two new instruments. Each had its own
statistical personality, and much of the R1 journey was working out
how to express both personalities inside a single coherent engine.

  INTARIAN_PEPPER_ROOT  (PEPPER, position limit ±80)
    An ARIMA(0,1,1) process with a small but persistent positive drift
    of roughly +0.10 ticks per timestamp. Over a full 10,000-tick day
    this compounds to +1,000 ticks of directional movement. This is
    not noise — it is deterministic carry alpha. Whatever I did here,
    I had to be riding this drift, not fighting it.

  ASH_COATED_OSMIUM  (OSMIUM, position limit ±80)
    An ARIMA(0,1,1) process with essentially zero drift. Mean ≈ 10,000,
    intra-day std ≈ 5, range-bound and mean-reverting.

Given the two very different generating processes, each product needed
its own fair-value framework. PEPPER wants prediction (forecast ahead
of the EMA lag, ride the carry); OSMIUM wants robust estimation (a
slow, noise-insensitive level). Pushing these two goals through a
common infrastructure — and keeping them from stepping on each other —
was the dominant engineering problem of the round.

The R1 journey spans 36 distinct iterations. That is more than most
people would realistically write for a single round, and I should be
honest that many were tiny parameter sweeps saved as new files to
preserve a clean diff history. Grouped by conceptual epoch the 36
files reduce to five phases.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R1 EPOCH A — ADAPTING THE TUTORIAL FRAMEWORK (v1–v6)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

R1 v1 is a direct port of Tutorial v6, retuned with R1-specific ACF
analysis:

  PEPPER:
    ACF(1) of ΔY_t ≈ −0.501 (vs. −0.57 on Tutorial TOMATOES).
    → optimal EMA α ≈ 0.50 (vs. 0.43 on TOMATOES).
    Slow EMA α = 0.06, trend-carry weight = 0.25 (inherited from v6).
    Against-trend passive capacity cap = 0.55 (throttle sells harder
    in an uptrend, since PEPPER is always uptrending).
    MOMENTUM_BIAS = 40, scaled up from Tutorial's 5 to match PEPPER's
    ≈ 12-tick spread (TOMATOES had ≈ 2 ticks, so position units per
    tick of signal need to be roughly 6× larger).

  OSMIUM:
    EMA α = 0.50 of the microprice, no fixed anchor. Working hypothesis
    was "ARIMA(0,1,1) with zero drift" — not stationary in level
    (there IS a unit root) but no directional bias either. The
    conditional mean IS the current EMA.
    Inventory skew quantised to ±1 tick at the position limit.
    No trend signal, no momentum bias, no throttling.

v2–v6 are a parameter sweep. By v6 the PEPPER EMA pair had slowed
substantially (α_fast 0.50 → 0.28, α_slow 0.06 → 0.015) and the
inventory skew had shrunk (0.025 → 0.015 per unit), with the urgency
multiplier raised (×2 → ×3). The intuition: on R1 data, chasing the
local mid with α = 0.50 over-reacted to tick noise. A slower fair
rides the drift smoothly; a smaller skew stops the bot from fading
its own carry. The urgency multiplier stays dormant until the
position is truly loaded (|pos| > 56), at which point it kicks in
hard.

I also dropped the two-level layering splits (L1_FRAC = 0.6 /
L2_FRAC = 0.4) down to a single price (L1_FRAC = 1.0). Tutorial logic
assumed competitor spreads might tighten to ±1; on R1 data,
competitor spreads were stable enough that a single penny price
beat the two-level split. The two-level logic was genuine insurance
that R1 data simply did not need to cash in.

  R1 EPOCH B — DRIFT CLOCKS AND WALL-MID (v7–v14)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The structural weakness of an EMA fair in a drifting market is lag:
by the time the EMA has risen to where prices are now, prices have
already moved further up. Epoch B introduced two ways to hedge that
lag.

  INNOVATION 8 — The PEPPER drift clock.

  If PEPPER drifts deterministically at ≈ +0.10 ticks per timestamp,
  I should not have to infer that drift from noisy price data every
  tick. I can anchor to the first valid price and linearly extrapolate:

      clock_fair_t = start_price + 0.10 × (t − t_start)

  The actual fair then becomes a blend:

      forecast_ema = 0.70 × ema_fair + 0.30 × clock_fair

  The blend weights matter. 100% clock is dangerous: if the regime
  changes, the clock becomes a lie. 100% EMA lags. 70/30 hedges model
  risk while still capturing most of the predictive benefit of having
  a deterministic drift model.

  INNOVATION 9 — Wall-mid instead of top-of-book mid.

  Top-of-book quotes can be thin — a single unit at 10,001 is not the
  market. A deep 30-unit quote at 9,998 is closer to where real
  business is being done. So I started computing fair from quotes
  with volume ≥ MM_VOLUME (= 18 for PEPPER, the typical market-maker
  size):

      wall_mid = average(prices where volume ≥ MM_VOLUME)

  When no large quote is present, I fall back to the plain microprice.
  This made both the EMA and the short-window OLS slope estimates
  substantially less noisy — the biggest unit-volume outliers in the
  order book are often spoof / post-and-cancel activity that shouldn't
  move my fair.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R1 EPOCH C — PREDICTIVE MODELS AND THE FIRST HARD ANCHOR (v15–v24)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

In Epoch C I pushed harder on prediction — both for PEPPER (where
drift is the alpha) and for OSMIUM (where the question is what level
to revert to).

  INNOVATION 10 — Short-window OLS for PEPPER.

  Instead of a hand-tuned EMA alpha, I regressed the last 6 wall-mids
  against time (a 6-tick OLS) to get a local slope estimate and
  blended:

      predicted = 0.70 × ols_fit + 0.30 × clock_fair

  The 6-tick window is deliberately short: noise at 6 ticks is still
  ±2, but the local slope is a close second-best proxy for the clock
  drift without requiring a hardcoded +0.10 constant.

  INNOVATION 11 (EVENTUALLY REVERSED) — Hard OSMIUM anchor at 10,000.

  OSMIUM's empirical mean across training days was ≈ 10,000.20 with
  std ≈ 5 ticks. If the true generating process really is a
  stationary OU / zero-drift RW around 10,000, then the best fair
  value is not the EMA (which chases noise) but the constant:

      OSMIUM_FAIR = 10,000

  Aggressive buys at asks ≤ 9,999; aggressive sells at bids ≥ 10,001.
  Locally this was great. It beat the EMA by a comfortable margin and
  I kept it through most of Epoch C and all of Epoch D. Only in Epoch
  E did I learn that this was the wrong long-run call — see below.

  INNOVATION 12 — Toxicity modelling for OSMIUM.

  Market-making OSMIUM at a fixed anchor is tempting until the book
  gets toxic: a heavy bid side or a one-sided microprice shift
  usually means one of my passive quotes is about to get
  adverse-selected. I modelled toxicity as:

      toxicity = TOX_OBI_WEIGHT × OBI
               + TOX_MICRO_WEIGHT × (microprice − fair)

  When toxicity on one side crossed a threshold, I scaled that side's
  passive size down by a fixed multiplier (≈ ×0.60). The idea: keep
  spread income on the clean side, stop funding adverse selection on
  the toxic side. I also introduced inventory regimes (NEUTRAL,
  LOADED, STRESSED) so that, when the position was already loaded,
  toxicity scaling got even harsher on the already-loaded side.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R1 EPOCH D — CARRY CORE AND FLOW (v25–v30)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Epoch D is the single largest change of mindset across all R1
versions. Up to v24 I was market-making PEPPER. From v25 I was
HOLDING PEPPER.

  INNOVATION 13 — Carry core and trading sleeve.

  PEPPER drifts up at +0.10/tick ≈ +1,000 ticks per day. If I simply
  hold 80 units for a whole day, the directional carry alone is:

      80 units × 1,000 ticks = +80,000 PnL

  That dwarfs anything spread-capture on PEPPER can realistically do.
  The risk is that symmetric market-making logic, by design,
  passively sells when the position is long — it is fading the very
  carry that is paying me. The solution: split PEPPER into two
  notional books.

    Carry core (68 units): strategic position. Do not sell except on
      a clear regime break. Refill proactively when the core is
      short of target (PEPPER_CORE_BID_EDGE = −1: willing to pay
      fair − 1 to top up the core).

    Trading sleeve (12 units): the rest of the position limit.
      Recycle normally against the spread, but only sell into fills
      at fair + 2 ticks or better (PEPPER_SLEEVE_SELL_EDGE = +2).
      Don't give up sleeve capacity for tiny edges — the sleeve is
      the spread-capture layer.

  This is deliberately economics language: I had two different
  "assets" (carry alpha and spread alpha) and I built two different
  policies for managing them. Symmetric market-making is right when
  the fair value is a martingale. When there is carry, asymmetric
  inventory policy dominates.

  INNOVATION 14 — Flow signal for execution pressure.

  Separately from the dual-EMA trend signal (which drives fair
  value), I started tracking the sign and magnitude of recent
  market trades — a buy-sell imbalance EMA with α = 0.25 and weight
  1.15 in the take threshold. A burst of market buys means other
  traders are aggressing upward; I should lower my take threshold
  on the ask side to fill before price has fully moved. Flow is a
  short-horizon execution hint, explicitly separated from the
  longer-horizon trend signal driving fair value.

  INNOVATION 15 — Regime hysteresis and adverse-carry confirmation.

  The risk of running a 68-unit carry core is that if the regime
  actually breaks, I ride that position straight into a loss. I
  needed a break detector that wouldn't fire on noise.

  Three signals, all must confirm, multiple ticks in a row:

      trend_signal < BREAK_TREND           (ema − ema_slow < −4)
      AND clock_gap < BREAK_CLOCK_GAP      (empirical − clock < −25)
      AND counter ≥ BREAK_CONFIRM          (3 ticks in a row)

  Asymmetric thresholds: 3 ticks to declare a break, 2 ticks to
  declare a recovery. Breaks require skepticism (carry is the base
  case); recoveries can be entered more eagerly. This is the same
  philosophy as a Bayesian prior that starts biased toward carry —
  evidence has to clear a higher bar to overturn the default.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R1 EPOCH E — ROBUSTNESS VS. BACKTEST OVERFIT (v31–v36)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

This epoch taught me the single most important lesson of the whole
competition: a local PnL improvement is not a real improvement
unless it generalises.

  v31 (the submitted R1 version) consolidates everything from Epoch
  D and adds one conceptual piece:

    INNOVATION 16 — Learned range anchor for OSMIUM.

    Instead of the hardcoded OSMIUM_FAIR = 10,000 from Epoch C, v31
    uses a blend of three book-relative signals:

        osmium_fair = 0.46 × wall_mid
                    + 0.20 × microprice
                    + 0.34 × learned_range_anchor

    The learned_range_anchor is a slow EMA of recent mid extrema. It
    converges to whatever level OSMIUM is actually trading at,
    without any hardcoded constant.

    This was strictly WORSE than the static anchor locally — by about
    424 PnL over two training days. I kept it anyway because I was
    not confident the official OSMIUM series would be centred at
    exactly 10,000, and I wasn't willing to put that constant into
    production without evidence.

  v31 also contains a final structural detail worth noting: the
  short-window OLS slope is still computed, but its weight in the
  fair-value blend is set to PEPPER_SLOPE_WEIGHT = 0.0. I kept it
  as a DIAGNOSTIC input — useful for the regime break guards and
  for log inspection — but took it out of the fair-value formula.
  An earlier version (v26) had folded the OLS slope directly into
  the fair and looked robust in backtests while quietly costing
  carry PnL. Signals have to be separated by role.

  v32–v36 (NOT SUBMITTED) revisited the anchor choice. I put the
  10,000 anchor back in as a 0.16-weight "Bayesian prior":

      osmium_fair = 0.46 × wall_mid
                  + 0.20 × microprice
                  + 0.18 × learned_range_anchor
                  + 0.16 × 10000

  Locally this recovered the +424 PnL. I backtested it on the
  official IMC engine during the late submission window.

  It was WORSE officially than v31.

  The lesson is blunt. A training-data mean is not a law of nature.
  Hardcoded price anchors embed an absolute assumption about the
  generating distribution, and the official environment does not
  necessarily match. Book-relative signals (wall_mid, microprice,
  learned anchor) all rescale naturally if the level drifts. A
  hardcoded 10,000 biases the fair toward the wrong target for the
  entire session if the actual level is somewhere else — and worse,
  it does so INVISIBLY, because every number in the formula still
  looks plausible.

  I submitted v31. It finished 153rd out of 5,707 teams.

  ROUND 2 — MAF MECHANICS, RANDOMISED VISIBILITY, REGIME STABILITY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Round 2 kept the same two products but added two mechanical twists
that the R1 engine did not have:

  MARKET ACCESS FEE (MAF).
    Every algorithm submits a single integer bid via a new
    trader.bid() method. The top 50% of MAF bidders see 100% of each
    order book on every submission; everyone else sees a random 80%
    sample of each book. Losing MAF bidders pay nothing — the "fee"
    is only the auction value of being in the winning half, not a
    real cash outflow. So MAF is effectively a free auction for
    better information.

  80% QUOTE RANDOMISATION.
    Each submission that loses MAF sees a freshly random 80% of the
    order book. This means two consecutive submissions can disagree
    about what the best bid even is, purely because of the
    randomisation mask. The R1 engine never did this, so R1 v31
    had no special handling for a one-sided book.

On top of that, the same products shifted in a predictable way:

  PEPPER is still +1,000 ticks/day drift, but each day STARTS at
    roughly +1,000 ticks above the previous one (day −1 ≈ 11,001,
    day 0 ≈ 12,000, day 1 ≈ 13,000). The intra-day drift structure
    is unchanged.

  OSMIUM is still ≈ stationary within a day, but drifts up roughly
    +8 ticks per day across sessions, with a consistent 16-tick near
    spread (e.g. 10,001 / 10,017 on day 1).

R2 was therefore less about new product models and more about two
things: exploiting the MAF auction, and making sure the R1 framework
didn't silently break under the randomisation mask.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R2 EPOCH A — FOUNDATION ON TOP OF R1 v31 (v0–v1)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

R2 v0 is R1 v31 plus the minimum needed to react to R2 mechanics.

  INNOVATION 17 — Fixed MAF bid at 200.

  I set trader.bid() = 200 as a hard-coded constant. From the R2
  briefing I inferred 200 was comfortably above the likely median MAF
  bid. Since losing costs nothing, slightly over-paying for the 25%
  visibility bonus is cheap insurance. I never dynamically adjusted
  it — not because I thought 200 was optimal, but because the
  information needed to calibrate dynamically (a stable estimate of
  the median competitor MAF) never appeared in-game.

  INNOVATION 18 — OSMIUM range-directional lean.

  OSMIUM is range-bound, so mean-reversion at the extremes is a real
  edge. Rather than passively market-making the same way everywhere,
  I compute a range_factor ∈ [−1, +1] describing where the current
  mid sits in the recently observed range, and shift my reservation
  price by OSMIUM_RANGE_SKEW × range_factor = 1.8 × range_factor
  ticks.

    At the top of the range (range_factor ≈ +1): reservation drops
      ≈ 1.8 ticks — I am actively leaning to sell.
    At the bottom (range_factor ≈ −1): reservation rises ≈ 1.8
      ticks — actively leaning to buy.

  The strategy stays passive (it never crosses the spread), but
  asymmetrises the passive quoting so I harvest mean reversion at
  extremes without paying any spread for the privilege.

  INNOVATION 19 — Hot-quote detection.

  At certain ticks I noticed the OSMIUM L1 bid would be 5+ ticks
  above L2 AND 4+ ticks above the near-bid EMA. That is not normal
  book depth — that is a transient aggressive buyer walking the book
  up. Similarly, a "hot" ask signals a transient aggressive seller.
  I maintain a hot-signal EMA (OSMIUM_HOT_SIGNAL_ALPHA = 0.20, take
  threshold 0.40), and when the signal crosses threshold I tighten
  my passive edge on the OPPOSITE side by 1 tick — capturing the
  fade when the transient aggressor inevitably pulls back.

  INNOVATION 20 — Toxicity decomposition (introduced in v1).

  R1's single-OBI toxicity got replaced by a decomposed score:

      toxicity = 0.58 × L1_imbalance
               + 0.34 × microprice_deviation
               − 0.08 × deep_imbalance

  The negative coefficient on deep_imbalance is deliberate: on R2
  data, deep queue pressure is often the OPPOSITE of short-horizon
  L1 pressure. Treating deep volume as CONFIRMATION (as v0 did) was
  mixing two different signals together. Subtracting it cleans the
  toxicity estimate. Empirically, deep imbalance correlates with
  longer-horizon mean-reversion pressure, while L1 imbalance
  correlates with immediate execution pressure. Opposite effects
  belong on opposite signs.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  R2 EPOCH B — ROBUSTNESS AGAINST 80% RANDOMISATION (v2)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The 80% randomisation mask occasionally wipes one whole side of the
visible book — some ticks I see only bids, others only asks. R1 v31
had no special handling for that case; the EMA would silently update
from a non-representative mid.

  INNOVATION 21 — One-sided observation weighting.

  When only one side of the book is visible I estimate a ref price as:

      best_bid + HALF_SPREAD   if only bids visible
      best_ask − HALF_SPREAD   if only asks visible

  where PEPPER_ONE_SIDE_HALF_SPREAD = 7.0 (roughly half the typical
  PEPPER near spread). I also scale the EMA and drift updates by
  PEPPER_ONE_SIDE_OBS_WEIGHT = 0.50. Half-speed learning prevents a
  randomly-masked one-sided tick from yanking the EMA toward a
  fictional level.

  The principle is more general: when the information source is
  known to be noisy, shrink the learning rate. A signal that's half
  reliable should contribute half as much to the state update.

  INNOVATION 22 — OSMIUM L1-only quoting.

  In R1 I posted OSMIUM passive liquidity as roughly L1_FRAC = 0.75
  at the best price and 0.25 one tick further. Under the R2 matcher,
  same-timestamp market trades fill only the FIRST matching order,
  so a second passive level is not real queue diversification — it
  just sits. I consolidated to L1_FRAC = 1.00, L2_FRAC = 0.00. One
  strong quote at the best price beats a two-level split in this
  matcher. A small but structural matcher-awareness improvement.

  INNOVATION 23 — Adaptive round-anchor for OSMIUM.

  The hardest lesson of R1 was that a hardcoded 10,000 anchor fails
  out of sample. But OSMIUM still needs SOME notion of its long-run
  level to express mean reversion. My solution, adaptive but stable:

      unit = OSMIUM_ROUND_ANCHOR_UNIT = 100.0
      round_anchor = round(first_valid_mid / unit) × unit

  On the very first tick of the session where I have a valid mid, I
  round it to the nearest 100 and treat that as the anchor. The
  session-specific anchor is inferred from live data, not hard-coded.
  If day −1 starts at 10,002, the anchor is 10,000; if day 0 starts
  at 10,008, the anchor is 10,000; if a future regime starts at
  10,098, the anchor becomes 10,100. The level assumption is
  WEAKER than "10,000 is the true mean" — it is "the session mean
  is near a round-100 multiple of the opening tick."

  The round_anchor is then used as an inventory GATE, not a fair
  value term. If the current mid is more than 6 ticks above the
  round_anchor, I refuse to add to long (can_take_ask = False if
  position > −32); similarly at the bottom. This converts the
  anchor into inventory PERMISSION rather than PRICE — easier to
  keep safe, harder to overfit than a fair-value weight.

  R2 EPOCH C — KINEMATIC TRACKING AND TAKER/MAKER SEPARATION (v3–v10)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

In this epoch I briefly experimented (again) with a fixed 10,000
anchor for OSMIUM in v3/v4, before the same lesson from R1 reasserted
itself and I reverted to the adaptive round-anchor. Two genuinely
new ideas survived:

  INNOVATION 24 — Alpha-beta kinematic tracker for PEPPER.

  A drifting price is effectively a (level, velocity) state. A single
  EMA tracks level but systematically underestimates velocity during
  regime change. An alpha-beta filter — the simplest kinematic
  Kalman filter stripped of its covariance update — tracks both:

      residual     = mid − fast_level
      fast_level  += α × residual                 (α = 0.35)
      fast_slope  += (β × residual) / dt          (β = 0.08)

  fast_slope is a cleaner velocity estimate than (ema − ema_slow),
  especially at regime breaks where the slow EMA is furthest behind.
  I use it as an extra confirmation signal in the adverse-carry
  guard:

      IF fast_slope < PEPPER_FAST_BREAK_SLOPE  (= −2.5)
      AND clock_gap < PEPPER_FAST_BREAK_GAP    (= −38)
      → early flat/short signal: flatten the PEPPER carry faster
        than the EMA-only guard would have.

  This is defensive, not aggressive. It catches waterfall moves
  where prices drop fast enough that the slow-EMA chain lags behind
  — exactly the scenario the R1 carry-break guard would most
  struggle with.

  INNOVATION 25 — Maker vs. taker reservation separation.

  A structural oversight in R2 v0 / v1: I was applying the range-lean
  AND the dislocation signal to BOTH the crossing (taker) fair and
  the passive (maker) reservation. But crossing the spread costs one
  full spread (≈ 4 ticks on OSMIUM). The range / dislocation edges
  are small — 1 to 2 ticks. Burning a 4-tick spread to "capture" a
  1-tick range lean is a losing trade on average.

  The clean split, introduced in v6 and kept through the submission:

      taker_fair         = fair − INV_SKEW × (position / limit)
                           (ONLY inventory — NO range lean, NO
                           dislocation, NO hot-quote shift)
      maker_reservation  = fair − INV_SKEW × (position / limit)
                           − RANGE_SKEW × range_factor
                           + reversion_bias_from_dislocation

  Crossing only on pure fair + inventory gifts. Range, dislocation,
  and hot-quote signals express themselves exclusively as passive
  quote placement and side sizing. This single split was probably
  the largest conceptual improvement of the whole R2 journey —
  small edges belong in passive quotes, not in take thresholds.

  R2 EPOCH D — DIP BUYER AND WHY I DROPPED IT (v11–v12 / final)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

v11 added one more speculative mechanism: a PEPPER dip-buyer.

  When drift > 0.035 AND no carry break is detected AND the clock
  gap dips sharply (< −18) AND the kinematic slope is weak (< −1.0),
  I incremented a dip counter. When the counter hit 2, I raised the
  PEPPER carry target by +8 units, buying slightly more aggressively
  into what looked like a transient intra-trend dip. In local
  backtests this looked great on the observed uptrend data.

  v12 — the submitted R2 version — REMOVES the dip buyer.

  The rationale is straight out of R1 Epoch E: any mechanism that
  increases size on the adverse side of a POSSIBLE regime break
  trades local PnL for tail risk. Even if the dip buyer is right
  80% of the time, the 20% where the "dip" is the start of the
  regime break costs more than the 80% of gains. Having already
  learned in R1 what happens when a locally-positive change fails
  officially, I stripped the dip buyer out.

    - Maker / taker reservation split      (INNOVATION 25)

  KEY LESSONS FROM R1 AND R2
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  8. Separate signals by role. In R1 v31 and R2 final, the dual-EMA
     drives fair, the flow signal drives execution urgency, the OLS
     slope is a diagnostic (weight = 0), and the kinematic slope
     gates the adverse-carry guard. Each signal has exactly one job.
     Mixing them — for example putting OLS directly into fair — made
     v26 look robust in backtests while quietly costing carry PnL.

  9. Inventory policy can carry real alpha. The PEPPER carry-core /
     trading-sleeve split (INNOVATION 13) is not a market-making
     improvement — it is a change in what I thought the strategy WAS.
     Market-making is symmetric inventory policy on a martingale.
     Carry capture is asymmetric inventory policy on a drift. When
     the process drifts, the second frame dominates.

  10. Hardcoded price anchors are brittle. The static OSMIUM_FAIR =
      10,000 was +424 locally in R1 v33 but worse officially. The
      same pattern repeated in R2 (v3 / v4 fixed anchor lost to the
      adaptive round-anchor). If an anchor is needed, DERIVE it from
      live data (wall_mid, microprice, learned range anchor,
      first-valid-mid rounding). Never hardcode a level.

  11. Crossing is expensive; express alpha passively when possible.
      R2's maker / taker split (INNOVATION 25) is the cleanest
      statement of this rule. Range, dislocation, and hot-quote
      signals live exclusively in the passive quote price and side
      sizing; they never enter the take threshold. Small edges
      deserve passive expression; only clean fair-value gaps
      justify paying a spread.

  12. Local PnL gains require official validation before trusting
      them. Both R1 v33 (static anchor recovered +424 locally) and
      R2 v11 (dip buyer) improved local backtests but were dropped
      from the submission. Official results confirmed the
      robustness-first choice was correct each time. Submit the
      version you can explain and defend, not the version with the
      highest local number.

  13. Randomisation hurts most when the model silently assumes full
      information. The R2 one-sided observation weighting and
      L1-only consolidation (INNOVATIONS 21, 22) are ultimately
      about not letting a random mask corrupt learned state or
      mis-size orders. When the data source is noisy, the learning
      rate should shrink proportionally.

  14. Prefer inventory gates to fair-value terms for level
      assumptions. The adaptive round-anchor (INNOVATION 23) does
      NOT enter the OSMIUM fair-value formula. It gates whether I
      am WILLING to buy higher or sell lower from the current
      position, which is a softer, harder-to-overfit use of the
      same information. A level assumption expressed as permission
      degrades gracefully; a level assumption expressed as price
      bias degrades silently.
