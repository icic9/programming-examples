My Trading Strategy Journey — IMC Prosperity 4 (Tutorial Round)
================================================================
Author: Ian Chan
Products traded: EMERALDS (stationary) | TOMATOES (drifting, unknown fair value)

This document walks through the four iterations of my trading algorithm, describing
the thought process, the improvements, and the lessons learned at each stage.

  TRADER (1) — First Attempt: ML-Inspired Heuristics
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

My starting point was intuition: I asked myself what signals I would use if I were
trying to predict short-term price movements in a fast-moving market.

For EMERALDS, I knew the fair value was around 10,000 (visible from the data), so
the strategy was straightforward: make markets around that price, take any order
that was clearly mispriced, and skew my passive quotes against my inventory to
avoid accumulating too much directional risk.

For TOMATOES, the fair value was unknown and drifting, so I assembled three signals:

  1. Exponential Moving Average (EMA) as my fair value estimate.
     I replaced the naive simple moving average (SMA) with an EMA because I knew
     that for drifting price series, recent observations should matter more than old
     ones. The SMA weights every point in the window equally — that seemed wrong
     intuitively. I chose alpha = 0.25 by feel.

  2. Linear regression slope to capture trending behaviour.
     I ran an OLS regression of recent midprices against a time index over a rolling
     30-tick window. If the slope is positive, the price is trending up, so I raise
     my fair value estimate slightly; if negative, I lower it. I project this slope
     forward by 3 ticks (the "horizon") and blend it into my fair value.

  3. Order Book Imbalance (OBI) as a short-term directional signal.
     OBI = (total bid volume − total ask volume) / (total bid + total ask volume).
     A positive OBI means more buy pressure on the book, suggesting prices will rise;
     negative means sell pressure. I nudge my passive quote midpoint in that direction.

  4. Inventory skew to manage position risk.
     If I am long, I lower my bid and ask prices slightly to make selling more
     attractive; if short, I raise them. This prevents runaway inventory accumulation.

The general structure — aggressive phase (take mispriced orders) followed by passive
phase (post limit orders around fair value) — was established here and kept in all
subsequent versions.

What I was not happy with: every parameter (alpha, window, weights) was a guess.
I had no principled way to know if alpha=0.25 was better or worse than 0.3. The
linear regression slope added noise for a process that might be a random walk.
I had no formal understanding of what statistical process TOMATOES actually followed.
This motivated a complete rethink.

  TRADER (2) — Econometric Foundation: Let the Data Speak
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

I went back to first principles and applied the econometric tools I had studied in
my LSE Metrics II course (Slide Packs #3 and #6) directly to the price data.

For EMERALDS:
  I ran a Dickey-Fuller (DF) unit root test on the price series. The t-statistic
  came out at approximately -97, which is an overwhelming rejection of the unit
  root hypothesis. This means EMERALDS is strongly stationary — it reverts to a
  fixed mean. I then checked the autocorrelation function (ACF) of the levels and
  found ACF(1) ≈ 0.03, essentially zero. This tells me the series is white noise
  around its mean of 10,000. The implication for trading is clear: the fair value
  is 10,000 with near-certainty. There is no point building a complex forecasting
  model; I just need to make markets around 10,000 as efficiently as possible.

For TOMATOES:
  The ACF of the levels showed ACF(1) ≈ 0.99, a classic near-unit-root signature —
  prices are highly persistent and drift over time. I then differenced the series
  (computed ΔY_t = Y_t − Y_{t-1}) and examined the ACF of the differences. I found:
    — ACF(1) of ΔY_t ≈ −0.41   (significant negative autocorrelation at lag 1)
    — ACF(2), ACF(3), ... ≈ 0   (cuts off sharply after lag 1)
  This ACF pattern — one significant lag then cutoff — is the textbook signature of
  a Moving Average process of order 1 in the differences. In other words, TOMATOES
  follows an ARIMA(0,1,1) process:
      ΔY_t = ε_t + θ · ε_{t-1}
  where θ ≈ −0.55 (estimated from ACF(1): θ ≈ ACF(1) / (1 + ACF(1)^2 ≈ ...) ).

  The critical insight: for an ARIMA(0,1,1) process, the theoretically optimal
  1-step-ahead forecast is an Exponentially Weighted Moving Average with smoothing
  parameter α = 1 + θ ≈ 1 − 0.55 = 0.45 (I used 0.43 after more careful fitting).
  This is not an arbitrary choice — it is derived from the Kalman filter / EWMA
  equivalence for MA(1) processes. I verified empirically that EMA(0.43) reduces
  forecast MSE by approximately 53% compared to SMA(20).

I also introduced the microprice as a better fair-value input than the raw midprice:
  microprice = (best_bid × ask_vol + best_ask × bid_vol) / (bid_vol + ask_vol)
This volume-weights the two sides of the book. If the ask side is heavier, the
microprice sits closer to the ask — a more accurate reflection of where the market
is likely to trade next.

This version also introduced inventory skewing and adaptive spread widening based
on rolling volatility estimates. However, in the implementation I introduced four
subtle bugs that reduced performance — these were identified and fixed in v3.

  TRADER (3) — Systematic Debugging: Rigour in Execution
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Even a theoretically sound strategy fails if it is implemented incorrectly. After
backtesting v2 and studying the logs carefully, I identified four bugs:

  BUG 1 — Aggressive fills used the skewed fair value for TOMATOES.
  At maximum inventory (position = 80), a skew of 0.15 per unit = 12 ticks.
  The aggressive threshold was checking: is this ask below (fair − 12)?
  This means I was refusing to buy at prices like fair − 11, fair − 5, fair − 1,
  all of which are genuinely profitable. I was leaving money on the table.
  FIX: aggressive fills always compare against the raw (unskewed) EMA fair value.

  BUG 2 — Inventory skew coefficient was too large for TOMATOES (0.15 per unit).
  At the position limit of 80, this produced 12 ticks of skew. The typical spread
  was only 4 ticks total. This meant my passive sell was priced 10 ticks below fair
  — I was gifting edge to counterparties. The skew should be small enough to stay
  comfortably inside the spread.
  FIX: TOMATO_SKEW reduced from 0.15 to 0.04 per unit → max 3.2 ticks at limit.

  BUG 3 — The adaptive spread was uncapped, growing very wide in volatile periods.
  With vol_adj up to 2 and inv_adj up to 2, the half-spread could reach 6 ticks
  (full spread 12 ticks). This was wider than the entire visible order book, meaning
  my passive quotes never got filled. The spread was supposed to adapt, not collapse.
  FIX: hard cap on half-spread at 4 ticks.

  BUG 4 — Aggressive fills for EMERALDS also used the skewed fair value.
  EMERALDS fair value is known with certainty to be 10,000. There is no reason to
  refuse buying at 9,999 just because I happen to be long at that moment. The skew
  on aggressive fills was causing me to miss profitable arbitrage opportunities.
  FIX: aggressive EMERALDS fills always use raw fair = 10,000; skew only applied
  to passive quote prices.

The core principle I established: the aggressive phase and passive phase must be
decoupled. Aggressive fills capture certain edge (mispriced orders) and should
never be skewed. Passive quotes manage inventory risk and can be skewed. Conflating
the two costs real PnL.

  TRADER (4) — Final: Market Microstructure Innovation
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

With a correct, theory-grounded implementation in place, I turned to the question
of how to compete more effectively against other market participants. I introduced
two new ideas from market microstructure theory:

  INNOVATION 1 — Book-Adaptive Pennying (queue-jumping).

  Instead of posting passive quotes at a fixed spread from fair value (e.g., always
  at fair ± 2), I read the actual live order book each tick to see where competitor
  bots are resting their passive orders. I then post 1 tick inside their best price.

  For example, if the best competing bid is at 9,997 and fair is 10,000:
    — A fixed ±2 strategy posts at 9,998 (same or worse)
    — My pennying strategy posts at 9,998 (1 tick above 9,997) to gain queue priority

  This is observation-driven: zero hyperparameters, zero risk of overfitting to
  historical data. It purely reacts to the current market structure. The only
  constraints are hard floors/ceilings so I never post at or across fair value
  (which would eliminate my edge). I applied this to both EMERALDS and TOMATOES.

  INNOVATION 2 — Two-Level Passive Layering.

  Instead of posting my entire remaining capacity at a single price level, I split it:
    Layer 1: 60% at the penny price      → tight price, queue priority, smaller edge
    Layer 2: 40% at penny price ± 1 tick → one tick further, larger edge per fill

  The motivation: if competitor bots tighten their spread (post at ±1 instead of ±2),
  a pure penny strategy suffers badly — you end up at fair ± 1 with zero edge.
  I simulated this scenario:
    — Fixed ±2 strategy when bots penny to ±1: near-zero fills (catastrophic).
    — Pure penny strategy when bots at ±1: posts at fair ± 0, negative edge.
    — Two-level strategy: layer 2 still has meaningful edge regardless of what bots do.
  The two-level split is more robust across the range of possible competitor behaviours.
  It sacrifices some PnL in the best case but avoids catastrophic failure in edge cases.

  The final version also carries over all bug fixes and econometric foundations from v3:
    — Aggressive / passive separation (raw fair for fills, skewed fair for quotes)
    — EMA(0.43) of microprice as the ARIMA(0,1,1)-optimal forecast for TOMATOES
    — Inventory skew on passive quotes only (max 2 ticks at position limit)
    — Spread widens by 1 tick when rolling volatility σ(ΔY) > 2.5

  KEY LESSONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1. Understand your data before choosing a model. Running formal statistical tests
     (DF test, ACF analysis) told me exactly what kind of process each product
     followed — and therefore exactly what the optimal forecasting strategy was.
     Intuition-based model selection (v1) leaves parameters ungrounded.

  2. Separate concerns in implementation. Aggressive fills and passive quoting serve
     different purposes; mixing them (e.g., applying inventory skew to aggressive
     fills) causes silent bugs that are hard to detect without careful log analysis.

  3. Small bugs, large PnL impact. Bug 1 alone (wrong threshold for aggressive fills)
     was causing me to miss fills at prices that were genuinely profitable. These
     kinds of implementation errors do not throw exceptions — they just quietly reduce
     performance, which makes them particularly dangerous.

  4. Robustness over optimisation. The two-level layering in v4 does not maximise
     PnL in any single scenario; it minimises the worst case across scenarios. In
     live competition where competitor behaviour is unknown, this robustness matters
     more than squeezing out the last basis point under idealistic assumptions.
